The Problem

It's 7:45am on a Tuesday. She's just finished a HIIT class at Gymbox Covent Garden, she's drenched, she feels virtuous, and she's heading to the fridge near reception. She picks up a Plenish green juice — £4.00, cold, organic. She notices the bottle: rPET plastic. She puts it back. Not because of the price. Because she spent last week reading about microplastics leaching into drinks stored in plastic. She grabs a water instead. Gymbox just lost a sale. Plenish lost a customer. And nobody in the cold-pressed category noticed.

The UK organic juice market is booming — growing at 7.3% year-on-year and now part of a £3.7 billion organic food economy. But the entire cold-pressed segment ships in plastic. Plenish: rPET bottles. Coldpress: their signature hexagonal rPET bottle. Juiceman: standard plastic. Every organic juice brand in the UK has made the same choice: invest in the organic credentials, then pack it in the one format their eco-conscious customer is actively avoiding. This isn't a minor oversight — it's a structural blind spot baked into every incumbent's supply chain and brand identity.

"Every organic cold-pressed juice brand in the UK uses plastic packaging — while 84% of UK consumers say aluminium cans are the easiest format to recycle. The contradiction is real, the gap is empty, and no one is walking through it."

Aluminium cans have an 81% recycling rate in the UK (Alupro, 2024). The average UK can contains 73% recycled content. Plastic sits at 30%. The Plastic Packaging Tax, now at £217.85 per tonne and rising annually, is about to make rPET increasingly expensive for competitors. And the UK Deposit Return Scheme — confirmed for October 2027 — will further elevate consumer awareness of can superiority. The window to own "organic + sustainable format" is open now. It will not stay open.

The Idea

Vita Press is a London-based organic cold-pressed juice brand that makes the aluminium can its primary — and defining — packaging format. Think Plenish, but for the consumer who refuses to accept that going organic means accepting plastic. Six signature blends, 250ml cans, 100% certified organic ingredients, zero preservatives. Produced with a commercial-grade Robot Coupe J100 Ultra capable of 100 litres per hour, and sealed in food-grade aluminium cans that keep juice shelf-stable for 12+ months.

The brand is built around the most media-friendly asset available to a small CPG startup: a genuine family story. A father-daughter joint venture, making organic juice in London, with no additives, no compromises, and a format that matches the product's values. The family angle is the content strategy, the PR hook, and the differentiator that no Britvic-backed brand can replicate. Distribution starts with London's premium gym chains, independent cafes, and weekend farmers markets — the highest-visibility, highest-conversion channels for a brand that needs to be experienced in person first.

How It Works
1
You pick up a Vita Press at your gym or local deli The can stands out immediately — in a fridge full of plastic bottles, the aluminium format signals something different before you even read the label. You scan the clean ingredient list: Beetroot, Apple, Ginger. That's it. £3.50.
2
You try it — and notice the format story On the can: "Infinitely recyclable aluminium. Made in London. No plastic, ever." The taste is cold-pressed quality. The QR code links to a 60-second video of the production process — two people, a commercial juicer, a real kitchen. You follow on Instagram before you've finished the drink.
3
You subscribe or reorder online The Shopify D2C store sells weekly variety packs and custom bundles. Subscription customers get 15% off and a free "immunity shot" (ginger + elderberry, 60ml can) with every delivery. Margin at D2C: ~70% gross. No middleman.
4
You become a returning stockist or bulk buyer For gyms, cafes, and delis: wholesale at £2.00–£2.20/can. For corporate offices: monthly case orders. For event catering: premium case rates. Each channel generates repeat revenue without repeat marketing spend.

Three trends are converging simultaneously that make 2026 the right year. First: organic food is accelerating, not slowing. The Soil Association's 2025 report shows organic beverages are projected to grow at 17.7% CAGR through 2030 (Grand View Research) — the fastest segment in an already-growing market. Second: the plastic backlash is structural, not fashionable. The Plastic Packaging Tax and incoming DRS create a legislative framework that will price rPET packaging up and reward aluminium brands over the next 36 months.

Third — and this is the proof of concept — UK beverage brands that built around the can format have dramatically outperformed their plastic-packaged competitors. Dash Water went from launch to £17.2M revenue (+42.1% YoY) selling canned sparkling water. Trip CBD holds 88% of the UK CBD drinks market in cans. MOTH Cocktails sells one can every five seconds in UK supermarkets. Liquid Death — the most obvious case study — turned canned water into a $1.4 billion brand. The format has proven it can transform commodity categories. Cold-pressed organic juice is next.

Proof Signals
🥤 Dash Water (UK): A canned sparkling water brand reached £17.2M revenue with 42.1% YoY growth in 2024, holding 40% market share in UK sparkling water — built entirely on the can format as differentiation.
💀 Liquid Death (US reference): Took canned water from $3M (2020) to $333M revenue (2024) and a $1.4B valuation. The premise: premium narrative + sustainability credentials + aluminium can format in a category every incumbent served in plastic.
📊 UK beverage cans growing 3.6–5.85% CAGR through 2030 — more than double the growth rate of plastic bottles (2.89% CAGR). Over 90% of UK consumers purchased a canned drink in 2024, up 7% from 2023 (Can Makers UK, 2025).
🌿 UK organic beverage market: Projected 17.7% CAGR through 2030 (Grand View Research) — organic beverages are outpacing the already-growing broader organic food category. Consumer demand for certified organic is structural, not cyclical.
🏋️ UK gym market reached £6.5B in 2025 with 11.5M members. Third Space (14 sites), Gymbox (10), 1Rebel (12), Barry's (9) — 40+ premium London gym locations where a cold-pressed juice at the right price point sells itself daily without marketing spend.
